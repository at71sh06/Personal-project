The Sustainability of Cars

Eleciticity powered

  • Powered by chemical energy usually stored in a lithium-ion battery
  • Propelled by electric motors
  • More than 10 million cars worldwide operate on electricity

Source: US Department of Energy

Hydrogen powered

  • A fuel cell is used to harness the chemical energy into electical energy
  • The chemical energy is generated by a controlled reaction between hydrogen and oxygen
  • Propelled by electric motors

Source: US Department of Energy

Gasoline(Petrol) powered

  • Most commonly used automotive fuel
  • It is a refined fossil fuel made from petroleum
  • It is powered by converting the chemical potetial energy from the fuel into mechanical energy with a combination of air and fuel by the spark-ignited internal combustion engine

Source: US Department of Energy

Diesel powered

  • Powers a fuel ignited internal combustion engine which convets the chemical potetial energy in the fuel to mechanical energy
  • Differs to a disel engine as in a diesel engines air is compressed before the fuel is injected
  • Among the most popular fuels used in the automotive industry

Source: US Department of Energy

Natural Gas powered

  • 3 different types of vehivles can be powered by Natural gas.Dedicated natural gas vehicles(that can only run on natural gas), Bi-fuel natural gas vehicles(which can on ether natural gas or gasoline), Dual fuel natural gas vehicles(which run on natural gas but use disel fuel for ignition assistance)
  • Natural gas powers a spark-ignited internal combustion engine.Thus coverting chemical potential energy into mechanical energy
  • It powers more than 23 million vehivles worldwide(Includes Liquid natural gas and Compressed natural Gas )

Source: US Department of Energy

Bio-diesel

  • Work in a similar manner to disel vehicles howerver not all OEM's reccomend using Bio-fuel
  • Biodiesel vehicles have the same internal combustion engine as typical disel vehicles
  • Biodiesel is made using organic materials, for instance vegetable oil, corn and sugar cane

Source: US Department of Energy
